Asian Americans

Asian Americans have been historically left out of and misrepresented in marketing creative. The following insights are based on resources we use at Google to create more inclusive portrayals of Asian American* people. Built with input from community partners, these pages are just a starting point and will change and evolve as we learn together.

Represent the diversity of ethnicities among Asian American people

Asian American people draw lineage from eight regions, 60 countries, and have vastly different national, regional, cultural, and personal identities. Creative work should reflect this range, rather than reducing all Asian American people to a single representation.

  • East Asian: People from this region may identify as Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Macanese, Taiwanese, and more.
  • Southeast Asian: People from this region may identify as Bruneian, Burmese, Cambodian, Filipino,* Hmong, Indonesian, Laotian, Malaysian, Singaporean,â€  Thai, Timorese, Vietnamese, and more.

    *Filipino people donâ€™t consider themselves Southeast Asian, despite the country geographically being part of that region. Some people use â€œFilipinxâ€? as a gender neutral signifier of people originating from the Philippines.

    â€ Singaporeans are a diverse population of Chinese, Malays, Tamils, Nepalis, and other ethnicities.
  • South Asian: People from this region may identify as Bangladeshi, Bhutanese, Indian, Maldivian, Nepali, Pakistani, Sri Lankan, and more.
  • Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander: People from this region may identify as Fijian, Hawaiian, Melanesian, Micronesian, Palauan, Polynesian, Samoan, Tongan, and more.
  • West Asian:* People from this region may identify as Bahraini, Emirati, Iranian, Iraqi, Israeli, Jordanian, Kuwaiti, Lebanese, Omani, Palestinian, Qatari, Saudi, Syrian, Turkish, and more.

    *West Asia is typically referred to as the Middle East, so people from this region may not self-identify as West Asian.
  • Central Asian: People from this region may identify as Afghan, Armenian, Azerbaijani, Georgian, Kazakhstani, Kyrgyz, Mongolian, Tajik, Turkmen, Uzbekistani, and more.

CONSIDER

Filipinos, Vietnamese, and Pacific Islanders are some of the most underrepresented groups of Asian Americans on TV, compared to the size of their U.S. populations.

INSIGHT

Many people identify as more than one race. A significant and growing portion of Asian Americans (excluding Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islanders) identify as two races (14.3%), and the majority of Native Hawaiians and Pacific Islanders identify as two or more (56.3%) races.

EXAMPLE

In Gran Torino, a movie about an unlikely friendship between a Korean War veteran and a Hmong teen, Clint Eastwood specifically cast Hmong people (both professional and amateur actors). It was welcomed by the Hmong audiences, and was a demonstration of being specific when portraying a group of people.

When developing your campaign:

  • Be specific. Build roles with a personâ€™s ethnicity in mind (e.g., Chinese, Samoan, Pakistani).
  • Consider calendar moments. Strive for Asian American inclusion in calendar moments beyond Asian Pacific American Heritage Month . Be mindful of how Asian Americans adopt or adapt customs for Western-dominant holidays like Christmas, and how these customs can differ depending on ethnic group.
  • Looking for feedback? Donâ€™t forget to partner with people and organizations who identify with the specific ethnicity you are trying to represent. Strive to have representation from a variety of these communities on your team and in production, whenever possible.

Identifying language matters

Be cognizant of the language you use to identify an individual. Asian, APA, AAPI, APIDA (Asian Pacific Islander Desi American), and Asian American are terms often used to more broadly reference Asian and Pacific Islander people. However, approximately two-thirds of this population identifies most strongly with their country of origin (e.g., Vietnamese or Vietnamese American). Ask what a person prefers â€“ if you canâ€™t ask, refer to the personâ€™s sub-region (e.g., Southeast Asian).

REMEMBER

When using a combined term such as Asian American or Filipino American it should be used without the hyphen. Using a hyphen can connote an otherness; a sense that the person is not fully American.

Consider age and immigration experiences in your portrayals

There is significant generational change occurring between those under 35 and those over 35: 80% of Asian Americans age 35+ were born in another country, while thatâ€™s true of only 34% of Asian Americans under 35.

Personal experiences will also vary depending on the time passed since immigration and the reason for immigrating. For example, a first generation immigrant may relate differently to U.S. culture than a 5th generation Asian American born in the U.S. Or, a person who came to the U.S. as a refugee may have different socioeconomic circumstances than an immigrant who came to the U.S. for higher education.

CONSIDER

Immigration has an effect on language use, accents, cultural norms (e.g., multigenerational household, music, food), socioeconomic status, and even profession. Consider these details when developing your work. Make an effort to discover and use real stories. Real stories are nuanced and nuance creates authenticity.

AVOID

Asian people in the U.S. are often portrayed as â€œforeignersâ€? with strong accents. If you feature someone who is foreign-born or identifies as an immigrant, never caricature them or their culture. Avoid making an accent or stereotypical characteristic the punchline of a joke.

EXAMPLE

The 2019 film The Farewell, featuring Asian American actress Awkwafina, depicted the conflicting views of a family of Chinese Americans on the sensitive topic of death. The interplay of generational and geographical differences among the filmâ€™s characters is a great reminder to consider how these identities can impact your audiences.

Represent the full spectrum of socioeconomic experiences

Asian Americans are the fastest growing group in terms of wealth in the U.S. and are frequently associated with STEM or professional jobs. Because of this, we often overlook the stories of those who donâ€™t experience the same privilege.

Income inequality among Asian American people exists. In fact, the wealth gap among Asian Americans is even greater than that of white Americans and disproportionately affects certain sub-groups. Marketing should reflect this breadth of socioeconomic and professional experiences.

Show an array of skin tones

Skin tone is an important dimension to consider in your portrayal of Asian Americans, particularly around roles, socioeconomic status, and hierarchy in your stories. There are long-standing prejudices that favor lighter skin color among Asian Americans.

For women, fair skin has historically been presented as a symbol of beauty and skin lightening products (also referred to as skin whitening or bleaching products) in Asian beauty markets have opened conversations around the harmful effects of colorism. Pay special attention to how you portray a womanâ€™s skin tone, and make sure it isnâ€™t consistently lighter than that of her male counterparts.

Build leading roles for Asian Americans

Inclusion and visibility in leading roles remains a significant barrier to representation of Asian American people. While roughly 6% of the U.S. population, Asian Americans represent only 2% of leading roles in film. We should look to feature Asian Americans in leading roles, and when possible, feature more than one Asian American at once to avoid tokenism and to reflect the geographic experiences of those living in the U.S.

REMEMBER

Representation is about more than just screen time. Ensure Asian Americans can tell their own story and portray Asian Americans in complex and substantial leading roles, not stereotypical ones.

AVOID

â€œWhitewashingâ€? and cultural appropriation of Asian people and cultures is a major issue affecting Asian and Asian American portrayals in the media.

Don't use Asian cultural or spiritual symbols out of cultural context.

Avoid typecasting Asian Americans in roles like the martial artist (unless it is a leading/central role) or the quirky sidekick.

Challenge exclusionary norms

Asian Americans often contend with cultural and religious traditions that may not embrace how they personally identify. Shed light on these intersectional identities: gender identity, sexual orientation, and disability are all characteristics to play up, not down. Consider the following topics:

Traditional gender roles & homophobia

There are some familial expectations such as protecting the family honor and respecting elders. This often leads to expectations for sons to carry on the family name and for daughters to take care of their parents in old age. In addition, the lack of representation of LGBTQ+ Asian Americans can often manifest into homophobia and transphobia. Show Asian Americans in a variety of roles regardless of gender expression or sexual orientation.

Interracial relationships

In romantic portrayals, interracial partners are more often shown, yet statistically less common, than same-ethnicity ones. Consider featuring same-ethnicity couples. Be extra mindful when featuring an Asian American woman with a white man as Asian American women were historically portrayed as sexualized partners to white men (see â€œexoticized womanâ€? stereotype in the next section). Equally, the historical desexualized representation of Asian men has meant interracial depictions with white female partners is still rarely seen. Carefully consider the context of representing Asian Americans in relationships and how desire and sexual characteristics are portrayed.

Racism

Interminority racism exists and is especially profound between Asian Americans and Black people. The â€œmodel minorityâ€? stereotype has long been utilized to downplay racism, especially toward Black Americans, and historical events like the 1992 Rodney King Riots exacerbated racial tensions between the two groups. The Black Lives Matter movement has sparked increased conversations addressing learned anti-Blackness among Asian American people and resurfaced the teachings of solidarity from the 1960s Asian American movement, born from the momentum of the Black Power movement in California. Marketing should strive to show that people of various identities can not only coexist, but also can â€“ and do â€“ thrive together.

Understand the context behind harmful stereotypes

Many Asian American stereotypes are rooted in geopolitical events dating back over 200 years. Theyâ€™ve become a common default in media, impacting everything from the way Asian Americans reach a â€œbamboo glass ceilingâ€? in corporate America to fatal violence against Asian Americans, heightened during the COVID-19 pandemic. We should be aware of these stereotypes and their negative repercussions so we can actively push against them.

Thief or villain

Following the migration of Chinese laborers to the U.S. in the late 1800s, Asian Americans became depicted as a threat to American livelihood, job security, and family values. Characters such as Fu Manchu and the Dragon Lady were introduced, perpetuating this stereotype in media.

Disease carrying

As concerns about increased Chinese migration rose in the late 1800s, the U.S. associated Chinese laborers with sickness, depravity, and filth. This same stereotype has been reignited due to COVID-19.

Exoticized woman

The exoticized woman, also known as the â€œfetishized woman,â€? or â€œsubmissive China dollâ€? is a stereotype that goes back to colonial times. With this stereotype, East Asian women are often portrayed to be sexually submissive, exotic, and traditionally domestic, and therefore a novelty to the white, cisgender men who choose to engage with them in sexual and romantic contexts. This China doll stereotype can be traced back to the late 1800s/early 1900s, and was exacerbated by depictions of U.S. soldiers finding wives or lovers during wars in South Korea and Vietnam, and continues in modern media today.

Perpetual foreigner

Following the Pearl Harbor attacks, Japanese Americans were forced into internment camps to prevent espionage, perpetuating the idea that all Japanese living in the U.S. were foreigners. This continues to find its way to the forefront of American media, with China being the topic of discussion at political debates. This can influence perspectives on Chinese Americans, despite Chinese Americans not necessarily having any connections or affiliations to mainland China and its government.

Model minority

After World War II, highly skilled Asian American workers were allowed to immigrate for careers in STEM fields. This propelled the myth of meritocracy that Asian Americans were able to overcome discrimination and achieve high-end professions with hard work and quiet perseverance. In 1987, Time Magazine featured â€œThe Asian-American Whiz Kidsâ€? which brought the model minority stereotype to the forefront.

Terrorist

South and West Asian Americans became the predominant targets of anti-Islamic hate crimes and profiled as â€œterroristsâ€? following the 9/11 attacks. Today, they continue to be featured as such in TV and film.

Social context

DO

Show a variety of professional and personal experiences of different socioeconomic levels; consider professions outside of STEM or the blue collar business owner.

Cast Asian Americans as leads in plays, TV, and film.

Consider casting multiple Asian Americans together. This also extends to romantic relationships.

Ensure people with heritage from underrepresented ethnicities are portrayed.

Amplify Asian Americansâ€™ intersectional identities (LGBTQ+, mixed ethnicities, disabilities, non-traditional gender roles).

Respectfully portray the nuances of Asian self-identity â€“ many Asians are proud Americans, others are proud immigrants to the U.S.

Create roles for Asian American men as the love interest or as desirable.

Cast Asian American women as authoritative without being domineering (which perpetuates the dragon lady stereotype).

Behavior

DO

Showcase a variety of personality types, including bold or assertive. This extends to relationship contexts, especially for women in interracial relationships.

Highlight stories where parents are portrayed in supportive roles, not limiting them to overbearing or distant ones.

Showcase interest and participation in a variety of sports and hobbies; including U.S.-centric ones such as basketball, soccer, or hockey.

Encourage the development of characters who may break societal norms or rules.
